What is a CCNA and why are you taking it?

The Cisco Certified Network Associate (CCNA) certification is an entry-level IT credential offered by Cisco Systems, aimed at individuals seeking to establish a foundational understanding of networking concepts and advance their careers in IT. Key objectives of the CCNA include mastering networking fundamentals, routing and switching, security concepts, automation tools, and troubleshooting skills. Reasons for pursuing the CCNA certification include enhancing career prospects, gaining foundational knowledge in networking, increasing earning potential, facilitating career transitions into IT, obtaining hands-on experience through lab work, and understanding Cisco's products, which are widely used in the industry. Overall, earning a CCNA certification is a significant step for anyone interested in networking and IT, reflecting a commitment to professional development in a dynamic field.

Wifi Deauther


How I made a homemade WiFi deauther out of a raspberry pi.

I've recently taken on an exciting project: I built a homemade WiFi Deauther using a Raspberry Pi! It was an amazing experience that combined my interests in programming, networking, and hands-on electronics work. To start, I gathered all the necessary components: a Raspberry Pi (I used the Raspberry Pi Zero W for its compact size and built-in WiFi), a microSD card, and some basic peripherals for setup like a keyboard, mouse, and monitor. Once I had everything ready, I began by installing the Raspberry Pi OS and configuring the network settings. Next came the coding part, which was both challenging and rewarding. I researched various libraries and resources to understand how WiFi deauthenticating works. I settled on using Python along with some powerful libraries that allowed me to manipulate the WiFi packets. The code I wrote essentially sends deauthentication packets to targeted devices, disconnecting them from the WiFi network. It was fascinating to see how networking protocols operate at a low level. I implemented a simple command-line interface to select the target device from a list of connected WiFi clients, making the tool user-friendly. After several rounds of testing and debugging, I managed to get everything working smoothly. I ensured I was in a controlled environment during my tests, as ethical considerations are vital in projects like this. In the end, I not only built a fully functioning WiFi Deauther but also deepened my understanding of WiFi protocols and network security. It was a fantastic learning experience, and I can't wait to explore more projects like this in the future!
